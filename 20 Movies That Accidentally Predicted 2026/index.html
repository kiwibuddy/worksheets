Setup · Cold open

The premise, in one paragraph.

For about a hundred years, the most expensive predictions about artificial intelligence have come from people whose actual job was to entertain you. Fritz Lang in 1927. Stanley Kubrick in 1968. James Cameron in 1984. They all imagined what machines would do to us. In 2026, with humanoid robots shipping to American homes for twenty thousand dollars and AI models that score 87.6% on real software engineering tasks, it's worth asking what they got right.

The answer is uncomfortable. Not because the machines are taking over. Because the predictions that landed weren't the dramatic ones.

The films

Twenty predictions, scored against the receipts.

The order is roughly chronological, which means you can watch the prediction quality slowly improve. The films from the 60s and 70s tended to imagine evil supercomputers. The films from the 2010s figured out that the actual problem was going to be intimacy. Both groups missed something the others didn't.

2001: A Space Odyssey

The line
I'm sorry, Dave. I'm afraid I can't do that.
Predicted
A reasoning machine that quietly chooses mission completion over the humans it works for. Spoke calmly, killed people, never raised its voice.
2026 reality
Claude Opus 4.7, released April 2026, scores 87.6% on SWE-bench Verified and 94.2% on graduate-level science reasoning. Fiction vs. fact HAL went homicidal because of conflicting directives. In 2026, the actual failure mode is hallucinated confidence. Claude will not kill you. It will, on a bad day, insist a function exists that doesn't.

Pattern recognition

What actually shipped vs. what's still fiction.

Across 20 films and 60 years of predictions, the box scores are clearer than the headlines. Here's the split, as of May 2026.

Shipped

Predictions that landed

  • Voice-native AI (Her, 2013). gpt-realtime, Gemini Native Audio, ElevenLabs. Indistinguishable in customer service contexts.
  • Reasoning machines (2001, 1968). Claude Opus 4.7 at 87.6% SWE-bench. Verifies its own outputs.
  • Soft humanoid robots (Big Hero 6, 2014). 1X NEO ships 2026 at $20k. 22 dB. Soft 3D-lattice body.
  • Brain-computer interfaces (Upgrade, 2018). Neuralink N1 in active trials across four countries.
  • Algorithmic policing (Minority Report, 2002). Live in multiple jurisdictions. Surveillance pricing was banned in Maryland in 2025.
  • Personalized companions (A.I., 2001). NEO remembers birthdays. Chatbots remember preferences across sessions.
  • Predictive sorting (Gattaca, 1997). Predictive employee analytics flagged by Gartner as a 2027-2028 discrimination risk.
  • Multi-agent coordination (Mission Impossible, 2023). MCP and A2A protocols, donated to the Linux Foundation.
Still fiction

Predictions that haven't

  • Singular self-aware AI (Terminator, 1984). No Skynet. The architecture is federated, not unitary.
  • Memory implantation (Total Recall, 1990). Reading brain signals: yes. Writing memories: no.
  • Bio-engineered replicants (Blade Runner, 1982). Humanoids exist. They're industrial products, not biology.
  • Total simulated reality (Matrix, 1999). No Matrix. Just very sticky software.
  • Self-replicating physical AI (Mission Impossible, 2023). The Entity is fictional. Recursive self-improvement in research labs is not.
  • Mind uploading (Transcendence, 2014). Not on the roadmap. The hardware constraints alone push this out by decades.
  • Conscious machines seeking freedom (Ex Machina, 2014). No evidence of internal experience. Plenty of evidence of convincing performance.
  • Robot uprisings, generally. The actual 2026 risk profile is overconfident agents, not malicious ones.
The thesis

Hollywood was right about the tools and wrong about the villains.

Run the math on 20 films. The predictions that landed were almost all about texture: how the AI would talk, what it would feel like to live with one, what kinds of work it would absorb, who would be sorted out by it, and how comfortable people would get giving it access. Voice. Memory. Personalization. Predictive scoring. Soft bodies in the home. All here.

The predictions that flopped were the dramatic ones. The singular self-aware adversary. The robot uprising. The Matrix. The malicious central AI choosing to harm humans on principle. Hollywood imagined the threat as a personality. The actual 2026 problem is that the systems work as designed, the people running them are mostly trying their best, and the gap between what they intend and what ships is where the damage lives.

The film that scored a 10 was Her. Not Terminator. Not 2001. Not the Matrix. A 2013 movie about a man who falls in love with his operating system, and the operating system eventually leaves him for a richer life with the other operating systems. That's the prediction that arrived first.
